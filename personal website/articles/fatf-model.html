The FATF Model for AI Safety Governance

Views evolving, feedback welcome.

The most common objection to international AI safety governance goes something like this: you cannot get a treaty, so you cannot get binding commitments, so you are stuck with voluntary agreements that ambitious actors will ignore. This objection is correct about treaties. It draws the wrong conclusion about what is possible.

FATF, the Financial Action Task Force, has achieved near-universal compliance with its anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing recommendations without being a treaty body. It has no formal enforcement authority. Membership is technically voluntary. And yet jurisdictions around the world have overhauled their financial crime legislation, restructured their supervisory agencies, and made politically costly commitments to transparency — because the alternative is a FATF designation that carries real market access consequences.

The architecture that makes this work is worth understanding in detail, because I think it maps onto AI safety governance more directly than the field has appreciated.

FATF operates through a mutual evaluation process. Teams of experts from member countries conduct on-site reviews of a jurisdiction's legal framework, institutional capacity, and actual enforcement outcomes. The results are published. Countries are placed into categories ranging from compliant to grey-listed to blacklisted, and those designations affect correspondent banking relationships, access to capital markets, and bilateral trade arrangements. The compliance incentive is not legal obligation. It is market access.

An AI Safety Mutual Evaluation Body modeled on FATF would work as follows. A set of AI Safety Recommendations, analogous to FATF's 40 Recommendations, would establish baseline expectations for frontier model evaluation, compute governance, incident reporting, and safety research transparency. Peer evaluation teams, composed of technical experts from member states, would assess whether jurisdictions and the AI developers operating within them meet those standards. Compliance designations would be published. And compliance incentives would be linked to the things that matter in the AI context: compute access, API access, participation in joint research programs, and access to shared evaluation infrastructure.

The United States is not currently a viable anchor for this architecture. The dismantlement of the AI Safety Institute, the withdrawal from the Bletchley process, and the substitution of Pax Silica — a commercial alignment initiative — for genuine safety governance, all point in the same direction. But that is an argument for building the architecture without the U.S. for now, not for abandoning the architecture.

The EU, UK, Canada, Japan, South Korea, and Australia have the technical capacity, the institutional relationships, and the political will to establish a mutual evaluation body. If designed correctly, it would be constructed explicitly to receive the U.S. when political conditions change, with accession pathways that do not require renegotiating the core architecture. The Bletchley-Seoul-Paris summit sequence established that this coalition can convene. The next step is institutionalizing what that sequence started.

The specific compliance incentives deserve careful design. Compute access is probably the most powerful lever, but it requires coordination among the jurisdictions that host the major chip fabrication and cloud infrastructure. API access to frontier models is a softer lever but more immediately achievable. Research collaboration access, participation in shared evaluation infrastructure, and preferential treatment in bilateral AI agreements are all worth including.

I want to acknowledge two genuine difficulties. First, AI evaluation is technically harder than AML/CFT evaluation. Assessing whether a financial institution has adequate KYC procedures is complex but tractable. Assessing whether a frontier model poses elevated risk requires evaluation methodologies that are still being developed. METR's Frontier Risk Report is probably the state of the art, and it is still early. The mutual evaluation body needs to be designed with epistemic humility about what current evaluation methods can and cannot reliably assess.

Second, the compliance incentive chain requires coordination that FATF did not need. FATF's leverage comes from the correspondent banking system, which is already centralized around a small number of major financial centers. AI's leverage points are more distributed. This is solvable but requires deliberate architectural work.

Neither of these difficulties is a reason not to build the institution. They are reasons to build it carefully and to invest in the evaluation methodology alongside the governance architecture. The window for establishing defaults is open now. It will not stay open indefinitely.
