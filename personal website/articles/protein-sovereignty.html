Protein Sovereignty Is the Energy Independence Argument for Food

Views evolving, feedback welcome.

There is a version of the alternative protein argument that talks about animal welfare. There is a version that talks about methane and land use and the $1 trillion economic opportunity by 2050. These arguments are real and I believe them. They are also not the argument most likely to move the people who are not already in the room.

Here is a different argument — one that has nothing to do with animals or climate, and everything to do with something that defense establishments, food ministries, and national security councils actually care about: not being dependent on supply chains you cannot control.

Conventional protein production depends on a supply chain that is long, fragile, and geopolitically exposed. Fertilizer for feed grain. Feed grain for livestock. Shipping for all of it. When the Strait of Hormuz goes sideways — and in 2025 it did — urea prices spiked 32% in a week. Countries that cannot produce their own fertilizer cannot buffer that shock. Countries that depend on imported grain for animal feed are one disruption away from a protein crisis that no amount of domestic livestock infrastructure can solve, because the livestock need the grain to exist.

Singapore knows this. Their 30 by 30 program, aiming to produce 30% of food domestically by 2030, is not primarily a climate initiative. It is a sovereignty initiative. Singapore is a city-state with almost no agricultural land and extreme import dependence. They have made a strategic decision that domestic food production capability, including through novel foods and fermentation, is a national security asset. They are right.

This is the argument that changes the conversation. Alternative proteins, particularly fermentation-based production, decouple protein supply from the conventional agricultural chain almost entirely. A fermentation facility does not need arable land. It does not need feed grain. It does not need fertilizer from a petrochemical supply chain. It needs inputs that can be sourced domestically, at scale, in almost any geography. A landlocked country, a city-state, a small island developing state — none of them can run a meaningful conventional livestock operation. Any of them can run a fermentation facility.

This is the food equivalent of what renewables did for energy. Before solar and wind became competitive, energy sovereignty was largely a function of geography. Countries with oil and gas reserves had it. Countries without them were permanently exposed to price shocks and geopolitical coercion. Renewable energy did not just decarbonize grids. It democratized energy sovereignty for the first time in industrial history.

Alternative proteins are doing the same thing for food, and the transition is just as early-stage and just as consequential. The countries that build fermentation infrastructure now are building a strategic asset. The countries that do not are extending their dependence on a supply chain that the Strait of Hormuz crisis made visibly fragile to anyone paying attention.

The philanthropic implication is direct. The donors and funders who have never been moved by welfare or climate arguments are not the wrong audience. They are an audience that has been given the wrong argument. Military readiness and supply chain resilience foundations. Food sovereignty-focused development funders. National security-aligned capital. These are substantial pools of funding that have legitimate, deeply held reasons to care about protein sovereignty and have not been adequately engaged on it.

GFI has started making this case, and deserves credit for it. The national security framing in their policy work has gotten more sophisticated. What is still underdeveloped is the integration layer — the collaborative architecture that lets Bezos Earth Fund, defense-aligned foundations, and food sovereignty funders all work on this together without duplicating effort or talking past each other. No one is building that right now. That is the gap.

The energy independence argument took decades to become consensus. The protein sovereignty argument is earlier, and the window for shaping the infrastructure decisions that will determine how the transition happens is shorter. The Hormuz disruption was a live demonstration. The question is whether the philanthropic community treats it as a signal or a news cycle.
