Global South Delegations Are Negotiating Blind, and EA Isn't Helping

Views evolving, feedback welcome.

Here is something that will be true by 2027 regardless of how the next eighteen months go: AI will be permanently on the agenda at the UN, G20, OECD, and every serious multilateral venue. The governance defaults that get set in that period will shape the architecture for decades. This is not speculative. It is how multilateral governance works. Frameworks established early generate institutional constituencies, compliance infrastructure, and path dependencies that make them very hard to reverse.

Most Global South delegations are not ready for this. I want to be specific about what I mean, because the vague version of this claim is both true and useless. The vague version is: Global South countries lack AI capacity. Fine. The specific version is: Global South delegations to UN, G20, and OECD AI discussions do not have adequate technical sophistication on the provisions that matter most for catastrophic risk governance. Verification mechanisms. Compute thresholds. Frontier model evaluation standards. The questions that determine whether international AI governance has any relationship to safety at all.

This is not primarily a resource problem, though resources matter. It is a framing problem. Existing capacity-building efforts — and there are several worth acknowledging — focus on AI for development, digital inclusion, and ethics. The Boell Global Majority Fellowship, UNESCO capacity building, various UN programs. These are doing real work. What they are not doing is equipping delegates to negotiate the specific provisions that the EA community cares about. The development framing is funded. The safety-governance framing for Global South delegations is not.

Why does this matter beyond the obvious fairness argument? Three reasons.

First, Global South countries have the largest objective stake in AI governance going well. The disproportionate harm from misaligned or misused AI will fall on populations that are least resourced to absorb it. The OMFIF, Brookings, and CGD literature on this is consistent and clear. The countries with the greatest interest in safety governance are showing up least prepared to shape it.

Second, they have genuine voting power in UN bodies. The General Assembly, ECOSOC, the bodies where AI governance norms are being established, run on one-country-one-vote logic. The math on this is not complicated. If you care about the substantive content of multilateral AI governance and you are not investing in the negotiating capacity of the delegations that constitute the majority of member states, you are leaving a significant lever untouched.

Third, the vacuum does not stay empty. Other actors with different interests are investing in these relationships. The countries and organizations that show up with technical support, capacity building, and partnership offers shape the positions of the delegations they support. That influence is not automatic, but it is real and it compounds over time.

I spent nearly a decade in rooms where this dynamic played out in environmental and crime governance. I watched delegations with strong technical support punch well above their weight. I watched delegations without it accept language they did not fully understand because they did not have the capacity to negotiate alternatives. The pattern is consistent across issue areas.

The tractability case is genuinely good. The convening infrastructure already exists. The UNTOC, UNGA, and G20 AI governance processes are active and accessible. The technical content that delegations need is not classified or proprietary. The gap is not the substance. It is the pipeline that gets the substance to the right people at the right time.

EA has researchers, funders, and frameworks. It does not have, as far as I can tell, a serious program for getting safety-focused AI governance capacity to the delegations that need it most and have the most votes. That seems like something worth fixing before the defaults get set.
