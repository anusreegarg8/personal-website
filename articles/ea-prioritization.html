EA's Prioritization Framework Is Running on Stale Data

 Views evolving, feedback welcome.

There is a particular kind of error that is hard to catch because it does not look like an error. It looks like careful, evidence-based reasoning. The ITN framework — Importance, Tractability, Neglectedness — is probably the most rigorous cause-prioritization tool in philanthropy. It is also, right now, producing outputs calibrated to a world that no longer exists.

Here is the problem. Every ITN score implicitly prices in a counterfactual baseline: what is the government already funding? Neglectedness is defined relative to that baseline. If a domain is well-resourced by public funding, it scores low on neglectedness, and philanthropic capital should probably go elsewhere. This logic is sound. The baseline it is running on is not.

In FY2024 to FY2025, democracy and governance programming saw roughly a 75% cut in U.S. budgetary obligations. USAID was functionally eliminated. The State Department lost significant capacity across bureaus with direct relevance to AI governance, international environmental law, and multilateral negotiation. V-Dem formally downgraded the United States from liberal to electoral democracy for the first time in its history. Freedom House dropped the U.S. score three points in a single year, explicitly citing anticorruption failures.

These are not marginal changes to the funding landscape. They are structural. And EA has not rerun the math.

Entire domains that were previously scored as adequately government-funded, and therefore deprioritized by philanthropic capital, may now clear the bar. The question is not whether these domains are important. They clearly are. The question is whether EA's neglectedness scores have been updated to reflect the 2026 funding environment, or whether grantmakers are still routing capital based on a 2024 baseline that was dismantled over the course of about eighteen months.

The ITN framing is a good tool that requires live inputs to produce accurate outputs, and the inputs have changed dramatically. A model that told you in 2024 that democracy and governance infrastructure was adequately funded is now telling you the same thing, even though the funding collapsed. That is not a failure of the framework. It is a failure to update it.

The community that built ITN reasoning is unusually well-positioned to do this work. The methodology exists. The research capacity exists. What seems to be missing is an organized effort to treat the 2025–2026 federal collapse as a data event requiring systematic neglectedness rescoring across affected cause areas.

There is also a human capital dimension worth naming. The people who ran the portfolios that just got cut are not retired. They are mid-career, security-cleared, and multilateral-fluent. Many of them ran the exact programs that EA's theory of change assumes will continue operating. They are, right now, in a 12–18 month window before private sector positions absorb them permanently. They are also the most accurate diagnostic instrument available for figuring out which domains are now genuinely neglected and which ones have adequate substitute coverage.

The field built a good tool, but the outputs need to be rerun. That seems like the kind of thing the EA community is unusually capable of doing quickly, if it decides to.

While, I do not know exactly what the rescored landscape looks like, I hope someone is finding out.
