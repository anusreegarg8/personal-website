The Grantmaker Bottleneck Has a Pipeline Problem and a Closing Window

Views evolving, feedback welcome.

Luke Muehlhauser documented something a few years ago that the field has not fully absorbed: there are roughly 30 to 60 active AI safety grantmakers globally, and the binding constraint is not money. It is seniority. Identifying the right interventions, vetting the right people, creating the right opportunities, making judgment calls on technical work you cannot fully evaluate yourself. That is not a junior generalist job. It is a job that requires having been around long enough to know what you do not know, and to have built the network that compensates for it.

The solution the field has largely pursued is hiring more junior generalists and hoping they develop into senior grantmakers. This works eventually. It is also slow, and we are in a moment that does not have a lot of patience for slow.

There is a different pipeline sitting largely untouched. The 2025–2026 federal collapse produced a cohort of displaced senior professionals who ran exactly the portfolios EA cares about. Not interns. Not people three years out of graduate school. People who managed $20–50 million federal grantmaking portfolios, coordinated interagency coalitions on AI-adjacent policy, represented the U.S. at multilateral venues where AI governance is being shaped, and built the enforcement and monitoring infrastructure that underpins everything else.

They are not retired. This is the part worth emphasizing. They are 35–55, mid-career, still highly motivated, and in a window that is closing. Within 12 to 18 months, most of them will have taken positions in the private sector, consulting firms, or think tanks where their government relationships get redirected toward business development. Once that happens, their utility for AI safety governance purposes is not gone, but it is substantially reduced. The relationships, the clearances, the institutional knowledge, the access to the rooms where things are decided — these things attenuate once someone has been out of government for two years and is optimizing for a different client's interests.

What would it actually take to convert this cohort into EA-aligned grantmakers? Not much, compared to building from scratch. They already understand portfolio design, grantee management, performance assessment, and compliance. What they need is orientation to EA cause prioritization frameworks, mentorship from existing senior grantmakers, and an organization willing to bet on adjacent expertise rather than waiting for the ideal candidate who spent the last decade in EA institutions.

The selective conversion fellowship idea is worth taking seriously. Not a broad pipeline for anyone who was ever a federal employee. A targeted cohort, selected on specific criteria: deep subject matter expertise in AI-adjacent domains, demonstrated judgment in complex grantmaking environments, and the kind of disposition toward uncertainty and independent reasoning that EA grantmaking actually requires. That last criterion matters a lot. Federal grantmaking culture varies widely. Some people optimized for compliance and process. Some people optimized for outcomes and drove their teams crazy doing it. The latter is who you want.

There is also a second-order benefit worth naming. These people are the diagnostic instrument for the stale-baseline problem I wrote about separately. They know which federal programs actually mattered and which were institutional theater. They can tell you, from direct operational experience, which domains are now genuinely neglected and which have adequate substitute coverage. That knowledge is not available from the outside. It requires having been inside.

The bottleneck is real and well-documented. The pipeline exists and is time-limited. The question is whether the field moves on this in the next 12 months or reads about it in a retrospective five years from now and wishes it had.
